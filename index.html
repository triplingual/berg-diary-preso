Between these two photos, Miriam spent just over two years imprisoned in the Warsaw Ghetto, which measured 1.3 square miles in area and was the Nazi-mandated home to somewhere from 400,000 to 450,000 people identified by the Nazis as Jews (or sufficiently Jew-like). Between the raw numbers of people and the residential stock destroyed by the German siege of Warsaw in fall of 1939, the mean number of people per residential room was 7.2. All statistics lie, and this one neglects, for instance, to tell the story of the thousands of families or individuals living (and dying) on the streets. Berg, however, kept a diary beginning on her 15th birthday and located many of the events she witnessed or heard about. ***move to next slide*** My project is to map and contextualize Berg's location-specific information.

Alternate interface to emphasize entries' focus.

Mary Berg didn't live in Poland, and she didn't live in Warsaw. She lived at 41 Sienna Street, took drawing classes at 13 Leszno Street, sunbathed on the roof of 20 Chłodna Street. Likewise, she didn't live in a 4-year collapsed time. She lived by the day, by the hour — especially true when you consider that she couldn't always be sure that she would live to see the next day, or the next minute.

Why I'm Doing This

To Show Both Time and Space in the Ghetto Experience

When this is all done, my hope is that at least some people using the site will have a greater appreciation for the day-to-day grind of the Warsaw Ghetto, for the fact that it wasn't merely a segregating experience but a terrorizing one over a long period of time.

As well, I hope the essay will show Berg's work as both an important resistance effort as well as a documentation of other small, individual resistance efforts.
